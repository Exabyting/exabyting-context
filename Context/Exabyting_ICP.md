# 1. Abstract — ICP at a Glance

*The summary below is the operational core. Everything after Section 1 is the supporting detail that justifies and extends it.*

### Who Exabyting sells to

Exabyting is a best fit for **digitally active, mid-market and scaling organisations** that have more software work than internal engineering capacity, and are comfortable extending their teams with vetted remote talent. The qualifying band is **~30–2,000 employees** (sweet spot 80–750), **$5M–$1B revenue** (sweet spot $20M–$250M), with an existing engineering/IT function to absorb augmented talent — or a funded startup actively building product.

### The four primary segments

| **#** | **Segment** | **Who they are** | **Lead service entry point** | **Core trigger** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| S1 | Software / SaaS scale-ups | Product companies with a roadmap outpacing hiring | Staff Augmentation | New funding, missed roadmap, hiring surge |
| S2 | Digital agencies & consultancies | Firms needing flexible delivery / white-label capacity | Dedicated team augmentation | New client win, bench-cost pressure |
| S3 | Mid-market digital transformation | Non-tech-native firms modernising (mfg, logistics, health, retail, fin) | Custom Software / Product R&E / AI&ML | DX budget, legacy EOL, new CIO/CDO |
| S4 | Funded startups (MVP build) | Seed–Series A teams building fast on limited runway | Product Research & Engineering / Custom Dev | Just raised, pre-launch, co-founder gap |

### How to qualify in 20 seconds

* **Fit** — In the size/revenue band, digitally active, reachable economic buyer (CTO / VP Eng / IT Director / founder).
* **Capacity** — Funded or profitable; budget owner exists; not distressed or in a freeze.
* **Timing** — At least one active trigger (funding, hiring spike, leadership change, legacy EOL, product launch).
* **Access** — Buyer/champion is identifiable and engageable on LinkedIn/email. Open to distributed delivery.

|  |
| --- |
| **Disqualify fast (anti-ICP)**  Micro-businesses with no engineering leadership; in-house-only cultures that reject augmentation; hard offshore bans / strict on-prem-only data residency that Lithuania+Dhaka cannot satisfy; one-off ‘cheapest freelancer’ buyers; distressed or hiring-frozen accounts; sub-$2k disposable project budgets. |

### Where to focus outbound first

Prioritise **Tier A accounts** (ICP fit score ≥ 80, see Section 13): strong firmographic fit *and* a live trigger *and* a reachable buyer. Geographically, lead with **EU/UK/Nordics/DACH/Baltics (nearshore from Lithuania)** and **North America (cost-arbitrage offshore with EU-overlap hours)**. The fastest-converting motion is single-role staff augmentation as a land, expanding into pods, custom builds, QA, and DevOps.

# 3. ICP Methodology — The Definition Layers

A mature ICP is not one list of attributes — it is **five stacked layers**. A genuinely qualified account scores on all five. Outbound that targets only firmographics (Layer 1) wastes effort on companies that fit on paper but have no reason to buy now.

| **Layer** | **Question it answers** | **Example signals** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1. Firmographic | Is this the right kind of company? | Headcount, revenue, industry, geography, growth stage, ownership |
| 2. Technographic | Do they build/run software we can extend? | Existing eng team, stack (job posts/BuiltWith), cloud usage, dev tooling |
| 3. Buying committee | Can we reach someone who can say yes? | CTO/VP Eng/IT Director/founder present and engageable |
| 4. Behavioural / intent | Are they in-market right now? | Funding, hiring spikes, leadership change, product launch, RFPs |
| 5. Engagement-model fit | Will they work the way we deliver? | Open to remote/distributed talent; no hard offshore ban; budget exists |

**Rule of thumb for outbound prioritisation:** Firmographic + Technographic = *fit*. Behavioural = *timing*. Buying-committee + engagement-model = *workability*. Tier A accounts have all three. Fit without timing goes to nurture; timing without fit is usually a distraction.

Before segmentation, every target must clear this baseline. Segments narrow within it; they never override it.

## 4.1 Quantitative qualifying band

## 4.2 Qualitative qualifiers

* **Digital posture:** Software is core to operations or to the roadmap — not a marketing afterthought.
* **Capacity gap:** Demonstrable gap between software demand and internal supply (open reqs, slipped deadlines, bench gaps, modernisation backlog).
* **Engagement openness:** Comfortable with remote/distributed teams; no absolute prohibition on non-domestic delivery.
* **Decision reachability:** A technical or executive buyer can be identified and contacted directly.
* **Financial health:** Funded (Seed–Series C) or profitable; not in layoffs/freeze or restructuring.

## 5.1 Segments at a glance

* **By trigger:** The in-market event being played against (see §11). Drives timing and the opening line.
* **By geography:** Geo A nearshore vs Geo B offshore. Drives the proof points (overlap hours vs cost arbitrage).

# 6. Segment S1 — Software / SaaS Scale-ups

|  |
| --- |
| **In one line**  Product companies whose roadmap is growing faster than they can hire engineers. They buy capacity that plugs into their existing team and ships against their backlog — fast, vetted, and without the cost and lag of full-time recruitment. |

## Definition

Venture- or revenue-funded software businesses (B2B SaaS, platforms, marketplaces, fintech, healthtech) with a live product and an engineering organisation that is structurally behind demand. They already know how to manage engineers; what they lack is enough of them, quickly, in the right specialisms. This is the highest-velocity, most repeatable segment for staff augmentation and the natural beachhead for the whole portfolio.

## Firmographic & technographic markers

| **Dimension** | **Marker** |
| --- | --- |
| Headcount | 50–500 total; engineering 8–120 |
| Revenue / funding | $10M–$150M ARR, or Series A–C raised in last 18 months |
| Tech posture | Cloud-native (AWS/GCP/Azure), CI/CD in place, modern stack (React/Node/Python/Go/Java/.NET), active hiring |
| Operating signal | Public roadmap, frequent releases, multiple open engineering reqs (often long-open) |
| Org maturity | Has eng managers / tech leads who can direct augmented staff day one |

## Buying committee

| **Role in deal** | **Typical titles** | **What they care about** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Economic buyer | VP Engineering, CTO | Velocity vs burn; hitting the roadmap without over-hiring fixed cost |
| Champion | Engineering Manager, Tech Lead, Head of Platform | Quality of talent, ramp time, fit with their workflow |
| Technical evaluator | Senior/Staff Engineer, Architect | Code quality, seniority, communication, autonomy |
| Influencer | Head of Product / PM | Throughput on the backlog; predictability of delivery |
| Blocker | Talent/People team, Finance | ‘We hire in-house’; contractor budget vs headcount budget |

## Pain points (ranked)

1. Roadmap commitments slipping because hiring can’t keep pace with demand.
2. 12–20 week time-to-hire for senior engineers in competitive markets; reqs sitting open for months.
3. Fully-loaded cost of FTE engineers (salary + benefits + recruiting + bench risk) straining burn.
4. Specialist gaps (DevOps, data, mobile, QA) blocking specific initiatives.
5. Need to flex capacity up for a launch and back down after, without layoffs.

## Pain → value → proof mapping

| **Pain** | **Exabyting value** | **Proof to surface in outbound** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hiring can’t keep pace with the roadmap | Vetted engineers integrated in days, not months | Speed-to-staff vs typical 12–20 wk hire; pre-vetted bench |
| FTE cost inflates burn | Flexible capacity without fixed headcount | Cost-per-output framing; scale up/down on demand |
| Specialist gaps block initiatives | Targeted seniority in the exact stack needed | Stack-matched profiles; named specialisms |
| Quality risk from rushed hires | Embedded talent under your own leads + QA discipline | Test/automation capability; works in your process |

## Trigger events (buy-now signals)

* Funding round announced (Crunchbase/press) in last 0–6 months.
* 5+ open engineering roles, or reqs open 60+ days (job boards / LinkedIn).
* New VP Eng / CTO / Head of Engineering hired (capacity rebuild incoming).
* Public product launch, major release, or expansion into a new market/platform.
* Job posts mentioning ‘contract-to-hire’, ‘augment’, or backlog/scaling language.

## Service expansion path (land → expand)

**Land:** 1–2 augmented engineers on the most painful backlog area → **Expand:** Dedicated pod → QA/test automation → DevOps/CI → custom build streams

## Messaging angles

* **Roadmap-velocity —** ‘Hitting your roadmap without a 4-month hiring cycle’ — speed of vetted capacity vs in-house recruiting.
* **Burn-efficiency —** ‘Capacity that flexes with the roadmap, not fixed headcount’ — for the cost-conscious VP Eng/CFO.
* **Specialist-gap —** ‘Need a senior {DevOps/mobile/data} engineer this month?’ — narrow, named, immediately useful.
* **Launch-flex —** ‘Scale the team up for launch, down after — no layoffs’ — timed to a public launch trigger.

## Channels & cadence

LinkedIn (buyer + champion multi-thread) primary; email secondary; light cold-call for Tier A. Trigger-led opener referencing the funding/launch/open-reqs. 5–7 touch sequence over 12–18 days. Engineering-credible proof beats salesy claims; offer a vetted profile or a no-commitment intro call to a relevant engineer.

## Disqualifiers specific to this segment

* Strict ‘FTE-only / no contractors’ engineering culture.
* In a hiring freeze or recent layoffs (capacity is contracting, not expanding).
* Pre-product / no engineering managers to direct augmented staff (route to S4 instead).

|  |
| --- |
| **Example target shape (illustrative, not a named lead)**  A ~180-person Series B vertical-SaaS company, ~30 engineers, raised 4 months ago, 7 open backend/DevOps reqs averaging 70+ days open, new VP Eng started last quarter. Buyer: VP Eng. Opener references the raise + open reqs. Land: 2 backend engineers; expansion path into a QA pod within two quarters. |

# 7. Segment S2 — Digital Agencies & Software Consultancies

|  |
| --- |
| **In one line**  Firms that sell software/delivery to their own clients and need flexible engineering capacity to fulfil work — without carrying an expensive bench between projects. Exabyting becomes their elastic, white-label delivery engine. |

## Definition

Digital agencies, software houses, dev shops, and consultancies whose revenue depends on billable delivery capacity. Their core tension is utilisation: too little staff and they turn down work or miss deadlines; too much and the bench destroys margin. Augmentation lets them say yes to pipeline, absorb spikes, and access specialisms they don’t keep in-house — typically white-labelled under their own brand.

## Firmographic & technographic markers

| **Dimension** | **Marker** |
| --- | --- |
| Headcount | 20–300; majority billable delivery staff |
| Revenue | $3M–$60M services revenue |
| Tech posture | Multi-stack delivery; client-driven tech choices; project-based |
| Operating signal | Project-based hiring spikes, ‘subcontractor’/‘partner’ language, capacity ads |
| Org maturity | Delivery/PM function already manages distributed contributors |

## Buying committee

| **Role in deal** | **Typical titles** | **What they care about** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Economic buyer | Founder/CEO, COO, Delivery Director | Margin, utilisation, ability to take on pipeline |
| Champion | Head of Engineering, Resourcing/Delivery Manager | Reliability, white-label discreteness, ramp speed |
| Influencer | Account/Project Managers | On-time delivery to their clients; communication quality |
| Blocker | Finance / margin owner | Sub-contract cost vs billable rate spread |

## Pain points (ranked)

1. Winning more work than current staff can deliver — risk of declining or delaying projects.
2. Bench cost between projects erodes margin; hard to staff to peak demand.
3. Client demands a specialism (mobile, AI, DevOps) the agency doesn’t keep in-house.
4. Hiring permanent staff for a single project is slow and risky.
5. Need delivery to appear seamlessly under their own brand.

## Pain → value → proof mapping

| **Pain** | **Exabyting value** | **Proof to surface in outbound** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pipeline exceeds capacity | Instant elastic delivery capacity | Speed-to-staff; ability to say yes to more pipeline |
| Bench destroys margin | Pay-for-what-you-use, scale down between projects | No fixed bench cost; variable capacity |
| Missing specialisms | On-demand access to niche skills | Breadth across web/mobile/AI/DevOps/QA |
| Brand continuity | Discreet white-label delivery | Works under your process and brand |

## Trigger events (buy-now signals)

* Announced a major new client win or large project (press/LinkedIn).
* Posting multiple short-term/contract delivery roles at once.
* RFP wins or pipeline surge signals; ‘we’re hiring across delivery’ posts.
* Expanding into a new service line (e.g. adding AI or mobile) they can’t yet staff.
* Visible bench/utilisation stress (founder posts about growth pains).

## Service expansion path (land → expand)

**Land:** One specialist or a small pod for a single client project → **Expand:** Recurring resourcing partner across projects → dedicated multi-pod relationship

## Messaging angles

* **Say-yes-to-pipeline —** ‘Take on the projects you’re currently turning away’ — capacity as revenue enabler.
* **Kill-the-bench —** ‘Capacity that flexes between projects — no bench drag on margin’.
* **Specialism-on-tap —** ‘Need {AI/mobile/DevOps} for a client and don’t keep it in-house? Plug us in, white-labelled’.
* **Reliable-partner —** ‘A delivery partner that ramps in days and works under your brand’.

## Channels & cadence

Founder/Delivery-Director LinkedIn primary; warm referral and partner-network motion strong here. Position partnership, not transaction. Time outreach to new-client-win posts. Offer a trial engagement on a single role to prove reliability before committing to a pod.

## Disqualifiers specific to this segment

* Pure-staffing competitors / other augmentation firms (channel conflict, not a customer).
* Agencies ideologically committed to 100% in-house delivery.
* Sub-scale freelancer collectives with no margin model to protect.

|  |
| --- |
| **Example target shape (illustrative, not a named lead)**  A ~60-person digital agency that just announced a multi-month build for a named enterprise client and is posting 4 contract roles. Buyer: Delivery Director. Opener references the win and the staffing gap; land one senior engineer white-labelled; expand into an ongoing resourcing partnership across their project portfolio. |

# 8. Segment S3 — Mid-Market Digital Transformation

|  |
| --- |
| **In one line**  Established, non-tech-native companies (manufacturing, logistics/distribution, healthcare, retail, financial services) modernising operations with software, AI, and automation — but without the in-house engineering depth to evaluate or execute it. Exabyting supplies both the build capability and the specialist expertise they lack. |

## Definition

Traditional mid-market firms where software is becoming strategic but is not their origin. They have budget (often a board-approved digital-transformation mandate), real operational data, and clear ROI pressure — but a thin or generalist IT function. This segment buys outcomes more than bodies: custom builds, product research to de-risk investment, AI/ML to unlock data, and QA to protect reliability. Larger deals, longer cycles, higher ROI sensitivity, stickier relationships.

## Firmographic & technographic markers

| **Dimension** | **Marker** |
| --- | --- |
| Headcount | 200–2,000 |
| Revenue | $50M–$1B |
| Industries | Manufacturing, logistics/supply chain, healthcare, retail/e-com, financial services, insurance |
| Tech posture | Legacy/ERP core, modernising; data-rich but under-exploited; small/generalist IT |
| Operating signal | DX programme, new CIO/CDO, legacy system EOL, AI exploration, compliance/efficiency pressure |

## Buying committee

| **Role in deal** | **Typical titles** | **What they care about** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Economic buyer | CIO, CTO, Head of Digital/Transformation, COO | Business ROI, risk reduction, modernisation on time/budget |
| Champion | IT Director, Digital Programme Lead, Innovation Lead | Execution capability, de-risking the build, vendor reliability |
| Technical evaluator | Lead Engineer / Solution Architect (if any) | Feasibility, integration with legacy/ERP, security |
| Influencer | Operations / line-of-business leaders | Will the tool actually be adopted and used? |
| Blocker | Procurement, InfoSec/Compliance, CFO | Vendor risk, data residency, security, business case rigour |

## Pain points (ranked)

1. Investing in digital products without validated research — costly rebuilds and low adoption.
2. Sitting on valuable operational data with no system to turn it into decisions.
3. Manual, reactive processes (QA, maintenance, forecasting) that AI/automation could handle.
4. No in-house ability to evaluate which AI/build investments are real ROI vs hype.
5. Unclear requirements causing misalignment between business goals and delivery.

## Pain → value → proof mapping

| **Pain** | **Exabyting value** | **Proof to surface in outbound** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Building without validation | Product research & engineering before commit | Validate before build; research-backed briefs reduce rebuild risk |
| Unused operational data | Production-grade AI/ML on real data | Predictive maintenance / forecasting / vision use cases with ROI |
| Manual reactive processes | Automation + custom software | Efficiency/cost-reduction framed in their operational terms |
| No in-house capability to judge AI | Specialist expertise without a permanent data team | Highest-ROI use case identified and delivered first |
| Requirement misalignment | Structured discovery → aligned roadmap | Clear briefs that align business and delivery |

## Trigger events (buy-now signals)

* Announced digital-transformation initiative or board-approved DX budget.
* New CIO / CTO / Chief Digital Officer appointed (mandate to modernise).
* Legacy/ERP system end-of-life, migration, or visible modernisation push.
* Public statements about AI exploration, Industry 4.0, or automation goals.
* Regulatory/efficiency/competitive pressure driving operational change.

## Service expansion path (land → expand)

**Land:** A scoped product-research/discovery engagement or a contained AI pilot → **Expand:** Custom build → ongoing platform development → QA + DevOps + retained capacity

## Messaging angles

* **De-risk-the-investment —** ‘Validate before you build — avoid the rebuilds and shelfware that erode DX confidence’.
* **Unlock-your-data —** ‘You’re sitting on operational data you’re not using — here’s the highest-ROI first use case’.
* **Modernise-without-a-team —** ‘Specialist build + AI expertise without standing up a permanent engineering org’.
* **Adoption-first —** ‘Tools your operations team will actually use, because we research the need first’.

## Channels & cadence

Multi-threaded: LinkedIn to CIO/Head of Digital + email with a business-case angle. Longer nurture; lead with insight/ROI content, not capacity. Expect procurement/InfoSec involvement — prepare security and data-handling answers. Discovery/pilot as a low-risk land. Vertical-specific proof and language matter most here.

## Disqualifiers specific to this segment

* Hard data-residency/on-prem-only mandates that LT/BD delivery can’t meet.
* No DX budget and no executive sponsor — ‘interested but not funded’.
* Heavily regulated workloads requiring in-country-only staffing.

# 9. Segment S4 — Funded Startups (MVP & Early Build)

|  |
| --- |
| **In one line**  Early-stage, funded teams that need to build product fast on limited runway and can’t hire a full team quickly. Exabyting becomes their outsourced or hybrid build engine to get to launch and traction. |

## Definition

Seed to Series A companies (sometimes pre-seed with capital) racing to ship an MVP or scale an early product. Runway is the binding constraint: every week of delay and every premature senior hire is expensive. Smaller deals and shorter cycles than other segments, but fast-moving, founder-led, and a strong source of S1 graduates as they scale. Best served with product research to de-risk the build plus a small, senior pod.

## Firmographic & technographic markers

| **Dimension** | **Marker** |
| --- | --- |
| Headcount | 5–50 |
| Funding | Pre-seed (capitalised) → Series A; raised in last 0–12 months |
| Tech posture | Building MVP or early product; small/forming team; may lack a technical co-founder |
| Operating signal | Recent raise, pre-launch, hiring a first/second engineer, accelerator cohort |
| Org maturity | Founder-led decisions; minimal process; needs senior autonomy |

## Buying committee

| **Role in deal** | **Typical titles** | **What they care about** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Economic buyer | Founder / CEO | Time-to-launch, runway efficiency, getting to traction |
| Champion | Technical co-founder / CTO (if present) | Build quality, senior autonomy, not needing hand-holding |
| Blocker | Cash constraint / investors | Spend discipline; build vs hire tradeoff |

## Pain points (ranked)

1. Need to ship an MVP fast but can’t hire a full team in time.
2. Limited runway — every premature senior FTE hire is high-risk.
3. No or thin technical leadership; uncertain what to build first.
4. Risk of building the wrong thing and burning the round on it.
5. Need senior, autonomous talent — not juniors needing management.

## Pain → value → proof mapping

| **Pain** | **Exabyting value** | **Proof to surface in outbound** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Can’t hire fast enough to ship | A senior pod that builds from week one | Speed-to-MVP; senior autonomous talent |
| Runway pressure | Build without premature fixed hires | Flexible spend; scale with milestones |
| Uncertain what to build | Product research before/alongside build | De-risk the round; validated MVP scope |
| No technical leadership | Build capability that runs itself | Works autonomously; founder stays out of the weeds |

## Trigger events (buy-now signals)

* Funding announcement (pre-seed/seed/Series A) in last 0–9 months.
* Accelerator/incubator cohort membership (YC, Techstars, regional programmes).
* Founder posting about building, hiring first engineers, or seeking a technical partner.
* Pre-launch / ‘coming soon’ / waitlist signals.
* Non-technical founder publicly seeking a build partner / fractional CTO.

## Service expansion path (land → expand)

**Land:** Product-research/MVP-scoping sprint or a small senior build pod → **Expand:** Ongoing product development → grows into an S1 staff-aug relationship as they scale

## Messaging angles

* **Ship-faster —** ‘Get to MVP without spending three months hiring a team first’.
* **Runway-smart —** ‘Build with a senior pod, scale with milestones — protect your runway’.
* **Build-the-right-thing —** ‘De-risk the round: validate scope before you burn it on the wrong build’.
* **Technical-partner —** ‘A senior team that runs itself — for founders without a CTO’.

## Channels & cadence

Founder-direct LinkedIn + warm intros via investor/accelerator networks. Fast, informal, founder-to-team tone. Speed and seniority are the whole pitch. Watch for cash limits — qualify funding before investing heavily.

## Disqualifiers specific to this segment

* Unfunded / bootstrapped with no real budget (‘equity-only’ build requests).
* Sub-$2k disposable budget chasing the cheapest freelancer.
* Idea-stage with no capital and no validation.

# 10. Buyer Persona Library

Personas cut across segments. The same title behaves differently by segment, but the underlying motivations, objections, and ‘what good outreach sounds like’ are stable. Use these to brief SDRs and write persona-specific messaging.

## 10.1 The Technical Economic Buyer

*CTO · VP Engineering · Head of Engineering*

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **Drives them** | Shipping the roadmap, controlling burn, building a reliable team without over-hiring fixed cost. |
| **What they fear** | Low-quality contractors creating cleanup work; losing control of code quality; vendor lock-in; looking reckless to the CEO/board. |
| **Buys when** | There’s a clear capacity gap with a deadline, and the talent is demonstrably senior and low-management-overhead. |
| **Top objections** | ‘We hire in-house.’ · ‘Contractors slow us down.’ · ‘Quality risk.’ · ‘We’ve been burned offshore before.’ |
| **Disarm with** | Engineering-credible proof, vetted profiles, ‘works under your leads and process’, trial-on-one-role, timezone overlap (Geo A) or cost/overlap (Geo B). |
| **Don’t** | Lead with price or generic ‘we do everything’ claims. Be specific about seniority and stack. |

## 10.2 The Business / Executive Buyer

*CEO · COO · CIO · Head of Digital / Transformation*

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **Drives them** | Business outcomes — ROI, modernisation on time/budget, competitive position, adoption. |
| **What they fear** | Wasted DX spend, shelfware, vendor risk, projects that don’t get adopted, security/compliance exposure. |
| **Buys when** | There’s a funded mandate and a credible, low-risk path to a measurable outcome. |
| **Top objections** | ‘Is this real ROI or hype?’ · ‘Can we trust an external/offshore vendor?’ · ‘Will our team actually use it?’ |
| **Disarm with** | Business-case framing, validate-before-build, vertical proof, security/data-handling answers, a contained pilot. |
| **Don’t** | Talk in engineering jargon or sell ‘bodies’. Sell outcomes and de-risking. |

## 10.3 The Founder

*Founder · CEO (startup) · Technical Co-founder*

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **Drives them** | Speed to launch, runway efficiency, traction. Often emotionally invested and time-poor. |
| **What they fear** | Burning the round on the wrong build; slow vendors; junior talent needing management; losing momentum. |
| **Buys when** | Fast, when convinced of speed + seniority and that the team will run autonomously. |
| **Top objections** | ‘Can you move at our speed?’ · ‘Will I have to manage you?’ · ‘Can we afford it?’ |
| **Disarm with** | Founder-to-team tone, senior autonomous pod, milestone-based spend, product-research to de-risk scope. |
| **Don’t** | Be slow, formal, or process-heavy. Match their urgency. |

## 10.4 The Champion

*Engineering Manager · Tech Lead · Delivery / Resourcing Manager · Programme Lead*

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **Drives them** | Their own delivery looking good; reliable help that ramps fast and fits their workflow. |
| **What they fear** | Onboarding overhead, talent that needs babysitting, being blamed if it goes wrong. |
| **Buys when** | When made to look good — given easy-to-integrate, reliable, well-communicating talent. |
| **Top objections** | ‘Will this be more work to manage than it’s worth?’ |
| **Disarm with** | Fast ramp, clear communication, fit with their process, make-them-the-hero framing. |
| **Don’t** | Go over their head to their boss without bringing them along. |

## 10.5 The Blocker (handle, don’t target)

*Talent/People · Procurement · InfoSec/Compliance · Finance*

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **Drives them** | Risk control, cost control, headcount vs contractor budget politics, security posture. |
| **What they fear** | Vendor risk, data exposure, budget overrun, undermining an in-house hiring strategy. |
| **Buys when** | Never the buyer — but can kill a deal. Neutralise early. |
| **Top objections** | ‘This conflicts with our hiring plan.’ · ‘Security/data residency.’ · ‘Procurement process.’ |
| **Disarm with** | Pre-empt with security/data answers, position as complement to hiring not replacement, be procurement-ready. |
| **Don’t** | Ignore them until late. Surface and address their concerns proactively. |

# 12. Anti-ICP & Disqualification Criteria

Saying no fast protects capacity for accounts that convert. Suppress or disqualify on any of the following.

## 12.1 Hard disqualifiers (suppress)

* No engineering/IT function and no funded intent to build (nothing to augment or build for).
* Micro-businesses under ~15 staff with no software budget.
* Strict ‘in-house-only / no external talent’ culture as a stated value.
* Hard data-residency / on-prem-only / in-country-staffing mandates that Lithuania + Dhaka delivery cannot satisfy.
* Distressed, in layoffs, or in a hiring/spend freeze.
* Competing staffing/augmentation firms and freelancer marketplaces (channel conflict).
* ‘Cheapest possible freelancer’ buyers with sub-$2k disposable budgets and no quality bar.

## 12.2 Soft signals (deprioritise, nurture only)

* Right profile but no active trigger — fit without timing. Nurture, don’t high-touch.
* Interested executive but no confirmed budget or sponsor.
* Very early idea-stage startup with no capital.
* Single tiny one-off task with no expansion potential.

## 12.3 Watch-outs (qualify carefully)

| **Watch-out** | **Why it matters** | **Qualifying question** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Heavily regulated workload | May require in-country/on-prem staffing | ‘Any data-residency or staffing-location requirements?’ |
| Prior bad offshore experience | Trust barrier, not a dealbreaker | ‘What went wrong last time — what would good look like?’ |
| Procurement-heavy enterprise | Long cycle, MSA hurdles | ‘What’s your vendor-onboarding process?’ |
| Non-technical founder, no CTO | Needs autonomy + scoping | ‘Who owns technical decisions today?’ |

# 13. ICP Fit & Priority Scoring Model

A 100-point model that any analyst can implement in a CRM/spreadsheet to rank accounts for outbound. It deliberately weights **timing (intent) heavily** — for outbound, a perfect-fit account with no trigger converts far worse than a good-fit account in-market now.

## 13.1 Weighted dimensions

| **Dimension** | **Weight** | **Scored on** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Firmographic fit | 25 | Headcount, revenue, industry, geo vs §4 / segment band |
| Intent / trigger | 30 | Presence & freshness of triggers (§11); ≤90 days scores highest |
| Technographic fit | 15 | Existing eng org, stack match, cloud, build activity |
| Buying capacity | 15 | Funding/financial health, budget signal, growth stage |
| Persona access | 15 | Reachable economic buyer + champion identified |
| TOTAL | 100 | — |

## 13.2 Tiers & actions

| **Tier** | **Score** | **Meaning** | **Action** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| A | 80–100 | Strong fit + live trigger + reachable buyer | Priority multi-threaded outbound, high-touch, fast |
| B | 60–79 | Good fit, weaker timing or access | Standard sequence + nurture; re-score on new trigger |
| C | 40–59 | Partial fit | Low-touch / marketing nurture; inbound-priority |
| D | < 40 | Poor fit or anti-ICP signal | Suppress / disqualify |

# 17. Outbound Orchestration Playbook

How to turn the ICP into motion. Each segment gets a default channel mix, a sequence shape, and a primary angle. Tune volumes to capacity.

## 17.1 Channel mix by segment

| **Segment** | **Primary** | **Secondary** | **Best opener trigger** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| S1 SaaS | LinkedIn (buyer+champion) | Email, light cold-call | Funding / open reqs / new VP Eng |
| S2 Agency | Founder/Delivery LinkedIn, referrals | Email | New client win / contract-role posts |
| S3 DX | Multi-thread LinkedIn + email | Insight content, events | DX budget / new CIO-CDO / legacy EOL |
| S4 Startup | Founder LinkedIn, investor warm-intro | Email | Funding / accelerator / pre-launch |

## 17.2 Default sequence shape (5–7 touches over ~14 days)

1. **Touch 1 (Day 0):** Trigger-led opener — reference the specific event; one sentence of relevance; soft ask.
2. **Touch 2 (Day 2):** Value-specific follow — the one pain/value most relevant to their segment + a proof point.
3. **Touch 3 (Day 4):** Channel switch (LinkedIn↔email) — short, different angle, no repetition.
4. **Touch 4 (Day 7):** Social/credibility or a relevant resource (vetted profile, mini case, point of view).
5. **Touch 5 (Day 10):** Multi-thread — engage the champion if you’ve been targeting the buyer (or vice versa).
6. **Touch 6 (Day 12):** Direct, low-friction ask — ‘15 minutes / a vetted profile / a scoped pilot?’
7. **Touch 7 (Day 14):** Break-up / move to nurture; re-enter on next trigger.

## 17.3 Message construction rules

* Lead with their trigger and a specific observation, never with ‘we are a staff aug company’.
* One pain, one value, one proof per message — no feature lists.
* Match the persona’s language: outcomes for executives, specifics/stack for engineers, speed for founders.
* Always offer a low-friction next step (vetted profile, 15-min intro, scoped pilot), not a hard pitch.
* Pre-empt the top objection for that persona (quality / offshore-trust / cost) in the proof, not defensively.

# 18. Operating Cadence & Metrics

Make the ICP self-correcting. Track these and feed learnings back into the bands, weights, and segment priorities every cycle.

| **Metric** | **What it tells you** | **Acts on** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reply rate by segment & trigger | Which segments/triggers resonate | Reallocate prospecting effort |
| Meeting → opportunity rate | Qualification accuracy of the ICP | Tighten/loosen the qualifying band |
| Win rate by tier (A/B/C) | Whether the scoring model predicts wins | Recalibrate weights in §13 |
| Avg. deal size & cycle by segment | Where the efficient revenue is | Focus/portfolio decisions |
| Land→expand rate | Whether expansion logic holds | Refine cross-sell paths (§14) |
| Disqualify reasons | Recurring bad-fit patterns | Sharpen anti-ICP (§12) |

|  |
| --- |
| **Review cadence**  Weekly: prospecting metrics and trigger freshness. Monthly: reply/meeting rates by segment. Quarterly: win-rate-by-tier review and a formal recalibration of the bands, weights, and segment focus against real pipeline. The first quarterly review is the most important — it converts this hypothesis into a data-backed model. |
